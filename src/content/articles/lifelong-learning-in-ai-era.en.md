As AI grows increasingly powerful—with large models now stepping in and dominating complex cognitive tasks once exclusive to humans—an urgent question closely related to each of us emerges: **How should ordinary people cope?**

To maintain competitiveness in our careers, the core is building an "irreproducible advantage" in a rapidly changing environment. In the AI era, the core of this advantage is the **reconstruction of the ability and methods of lifelong learning**.

## The Failure of Traditional Learning Models

In the past, our learning path was often linear: spending over a decade in school "caching" knowledge into our brains, and then slowly "extracting" it to monetize over a decades-long career. This model relied on a hypothesis: **the half-life of knowledge is long, and the cost of retrieving knowledge is high.**

Generative AI (like ChatGPT, Claude, DeepSeek, etc.) has completely shattered this assumption. Now, any structured, known knowledge that can be derived through search and summarization can be presented by AI in seconds with accuracy and organization that surpasses the vast majority of humans.

If your pride still lies in "I know more than others" or "I have memorized many Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)," then your moat is nearly dried up. In front of AI, competing on storage capacity and retrieval speed is a unwinnable game.

## Reconstructing Your Learning Coordinates

Since "storing knowledge" is no longer the core of competitiveness, what should ordinary people learn in the AI era? And how?

### 1. From "Getting Answers" to "Asking Good Questions"

AI is a powerful "answer machine," but it does not proactively ask valuable questions on its own. One of the most important skills in the future is **Prompt Engineering**. This doesn't just mean writing instructions that AI can understand; it is a **logical thinking ability to dismantle, structure, and precisely define complex real-world problems**.

Learning is no longer about finding the standard answer, but learning how to ask the question that hits the nail on the head in a field full of unknowns. Asking a good question means you have grasped the essence of the matter, and AI merely helps you fill in the details from essence to execution.

### 2. The "Flint" of Cross-Disciplinary Knowledge

AI is already extremely strong in the depth of single domains, but in the blurry areas across domains, human associative ability still holds an advantage. The essence of innovation is often the recombination of old elements.

Lifelong learning should pay more attention to crossing boundaries. A programmer who only knows programming is easily replaced by AI, but a developer who understands programming, financial logic, and psychology can create trading systems or products that AI cannot independently conceive. Cross-disciplinary knowledge reserves are like flint; only when they collide can they spark the fire to solve complex real-world problems.

### 3. Master AI, Don't Compete With It

Do not try to beat AI in calculation speed and lines of code. The correct posture is to treat AI as your "exobrain" and tireless intern.

- **Use AI to accelerate learning**: When encountering theories you don't understand, ask AI to explain them using the Feynman technique; if you can't read long financial reports, let AI extract key data.
- **Let AI handle tedious labor**: Outsource data cleaning, basic code writing, and drafting copy to AI, and focus your energy on "review, decision-making, and strategic planning."

Your value lies in being a "commander," no longer a "bricklayer."

## Aesthetics and Taste: The Last Moat

When the threshold of productivity is drastically lowered by AI, and everyone can easily produce an 80-point piece of work (whether it's an article, code, or design), what becomes scarce?

It is **Taste** and **Aesthetics**.

Knowing what is good, knowing which details can truly touch people's hearts, knowing which of the many solutions provided by AI best fits the current business context—this is an ability that requires a large amount of life experience, emotional resonance, and cultural accumulation to cultivate. It cannot be learned through rote exercises; it needs to be slowly developed through long-term reading, thinking, experiencing, and even setbacks.

## The Inevitable Restructuring of Education Systems

When "storing and retrieving knowledge" no longer constitutes a barrier, the assembly-line modern education system will inevitably undergo a complete restructuring. The focus of future education will shift from "imparting certain knowledge" to "cultivating the ability to cope with uncertainty." As Andreas Schleicher, Director for Education and Skills at the OECD, aptly put it: "If we want to stay ahead of technological developments, we have to find and refine the qualities that are unique to our humanity... schools need to develop first-class humans, not second-class robots."

Similarly, reports from the World Economic Forum (WEF) highlight that in the AI era, information no longer differentiates people—**agency does**. Traditional standardized testing will gradually lose its meaning, replaced by Project-based Learning (PBL) and real-world problem-solving. Educators will also transition from "knowledge workers" to "wisdom workers."

Education will no longer be about making students skilled "operators," but cultivating them into "commanders": learning how to utilize AI tools, evaluate AI outputs, and integrate resources across disciplines. Concurrently, highly human traits such as resilience and empathy will carry much more weight in future education.

## The Rise of the "Super Individual"

The ultimate outcome of educational restructuring and AI empowerment is the mass emergence of "Super Individuals."

Recent data shows that frequent AI users experience personal productivity gains ranging from 20% to 40%. More importantly, the paradigm is shifting from "augmentation" to "automation." In the past, completing a complex project required a multifaceted team; now, AI can handle roughly 80% of routine knowledge work, leaving the super individual to provide the remaining 20%—strategic judgment and creative direction.

Gartner predicts that through 2026, 20% of organizations will use AI to flatten their structures, potentially eliminating more than half of middle-management positions. Super individuals are no longer constrained by physical limits or the boundaries of a single skill. Their core assets are **creativity, vision, and resource orchestration**. In the future, the traditional corporate pyramid may gradually be deconstructed, replaced by a distributed collaborative network composed of countless super individuals and AI agents.

## Conclusion
Personal growth is essentially a form of long-term investment: do not invest your energy in areas that are certain to depreciate (rote memorization of knowledge), but allocate it to abilities with asymmetric return potential (cross-disciplinary thinking, the ability to question, aesthetics, and taste).

Lifelong learning, in the AI era, is not a slogan, but a necessity for survival. The tide of technology never waits for anyone, but as long as we can adjust our course, AI will not be the flood that drowns us, but the massive wave that propels us forward.
